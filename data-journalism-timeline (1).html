1952

UNIVAC

In 1952, CBS News made a groundbreaking foray into the emerging field of data journalism during the U.S. presidential election between Dwight D. Eisenhower and Adlai Stevenson.

They utilized UNIVAC, one of the earliest computers, to predict the outcome of the election. This marked the first time that computer algorithms were used for predictive analysis in election coverage.

The process and its predictions were reported on by journalists Charles Collingwood and Walter Cronkite, with operational support from experts at Remington Rand, the manufacturers of UNIVAC. The successful prediction by UNIVAC of Eisenhower's landslide election is now seen as a seminal moment in the history of data journalism, signaling the start of a new era where data and computational power would become integral to journalistic inquiry and reporting.

1967

Precision Journalism

Philip Meyer's analysis of the 1967 Detroit riots stands as a defining instance of early data journalism, where he leveraged the scientific method to bring a data-driven perspective to investigative reporting. This event was a pivotal point in American history, characterized by widespread civil disorder and significant social strife.

Meyer approached the incident with a novel lens, applying quantitative analysis to understand the underlying causes and the dynamics of the riots.

For his study, Meyer surveyed residents and participants in the riots to gather data on their backgrounds, attitudes, and the events they witnessed or partook in. This approach enabled Meyer to test hypotheses about the causes of the unrest, much in the way a social scientist would approach a research question. His work not only provided a deeper and more detailed understanding of the Detroit riots but also laid the intellectual foundation for what would later be known as computer-assisted reporting (CAR).

1989

The Establishment of NICAR

In the 1980s, the seeds of computer-assisted reporting (CAR) began to germinate, culminating in a significant development with the establishment of the National Institute for Computer-Assisted Reporting (NICAR) in 1989.

This initiative, housed within the University of Missouri's School of Journalism and facilitated by Investigative Reporters and Editors (IRE), represented a unified effort to systematize CAR methodologies.

NICAR emerged through the vision of pioneering journalists such as Brant Houston, a leading figure in the CAR space, who foresaw the transformative impact of computational tools in investigative journalism. Houston, alongside Pulitzer Prize winner Stephen Doig and data editor David Donald, contributed to NICAR's mission, which focused on advocating for and educating journalists in the nuanced integration of data analysis into their craft.

2009

Wikileaks

The evolution of data journalism reached a pivotal moment in 2009 with The Guardian's release of "The Afghanistan war logs," orchestrated by journalists including Nick Davies and David Leigh.

This publication, derived from one of the largest leaks in US military history provided by WikiLeaks, included over 90,000 military officer reports that peeled back layers of the war in Afghanistan.

The Guardian's team sifted through the data, unearthing pivotal revelations about covert operations, the Taliban's support network, and underreported civilian casualty figures. The journalists transformed the raw data into user-friendly databases and interactive maps, giving readers an immersive experience in understanding the war's complexities.

2016

Global Narratives

The Panama Papers, revealed in 2016, served as a testament to the potent force of global investigative journalism. Orchestrated by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists (ICIJ), the project was a deep dive into 11.5 million leaked documents that laid bare the sprawling universe of offshore finance.

The investigation was collaborative, involving over 370 journalists from 76 countries.

These regional journalists were instrumental in contextualizing the data, which illuminated the ways in which political leaders, business moguls, and criminals alike obscured wealth and evaded taxes. The collaboration brought to the forefront region-specific stories that might have otherwise remained in the shadows, ensuring that a global perspective was woven into the narrative. 2020

The Pandemic

In the 2020s, data journalism took on a pivotal role in reshaping investigative reporting, especially evident during the COVID-19 pandemic. This global crisis presented unique challenges, and data journalism stepped up to provide essential insights.

Journalists worldwide harnessed the power of real-time data to track and analyze the pandemic's progression.

This approach allowed them to offer the public timely and critical information on key aspects of the crisis. They delved into the virus's spread, monitored death rates, and kept track of vaccine distribution. Furthermore, they shed light on the socioeconomic impacts of the pandemic, providing a comprehensive view of its far-reaching effects. This use of data by journalists was instrumental in keeping the public informed and aiding in decision-making during an unprecedented global health emergency.
